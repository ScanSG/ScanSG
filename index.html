🚀 You’ve Just Landed: Your First Steps in Singapore

Welcome to Singapore! The plane’s wheels have touched down, and you’re about to step into one of the world’s most efficient cities. Whether you're here for 3 days or 3 years, this guide will get you from the tarmac to your destination like a pro — no stress, no confusion.

🛬 Step 1: Clear Immigration & Baggage

Head to immigration with your passport. Automated gates for ePassport holders. Collect bags, then customs.

💡 Pro Tip: Check Singapore Customs for import rules.

🛍️ Step 2: Duty-Free & Essentials

  • Duty-free: Alcohol, perfume, chocolate
  • Convenience stores: 7-Eleven, Cheers (snacks, meds)
  • ATMs & currency: SGD is king. Cards accepted widely.

📱 Step 3: Buy a Tourist SIM

Available at counters in T1, T2, T3 after immigration or prebook a eSIM here.

  • Singtel – Fast 5G
  • StarHub – Great tourist packs
  • M1 – Budget-friendly

💬 Tip: ~SGD 15–30 for 5–10GB (7–30 days). Passport required.

🚇 Step 4: Get an EZ-Link Card

Your go-to card for MRT, buses, hawker centres, and convenience stores. Costs SGD 10 (includes SGD 5 refundable deposit).

📍 Where to Buy

  • Changi Airport: T2 & T3 MRT stations (Passenger Service Centre or vending machines)
  • Any MRT station: Service counters or Add Value Machines
  • Convenience stores: 7-Eleven, Cheers, Guardian (look for EZ-Link logo)

💰 Top Up Options

  • MRT stations: Add Value Machines (cash or card)
  • Stores: 7-Eleven, Cheers, FairPrice (cash or card)
  • Minimum top-up: SGD 5 or 10 (machine-dependent)

📱 Digital Tools

  • EZ-Link App: Check balance, top up online, transfer value at any MRT gate
  • SimplyGo: Skip the card — use your contactless bank card or phone (Apple/Google Pay)

💡 Pro Tip: Always tap in AND tap out. Forget to tap out = max fare. And remember: deposit is refundable at selected stations.

🚆 Step 5: Ride the MRT into the City

Take the East-West Line (Green) from CG2.

  • City Hall (~25 mins) – Central district
  • Orchard (~35 mins) – Shopping
  • HarbourFront (~30 mins) – Sentosa
  • Jurong East (~45 mins) – West

Trains: 5:30 AM – past midnight | Use TransitLink App

🧳 Traveling with Luggage?

  • Spacious trains & elevators at every station
  • Clear signage in English
  • Avoid peak hours: 7:30–9:00 AM & 5:30–7:30 PM

🚆 Master Public Transport

  • Tap in & out — always! Missing tap = max fare.
  • EZ-Link: Buy at MRT. More info
  • SimplyGo: Tap with contactless card/phone. Learn how
  • Apps: TransitLink, Grab, SGSecure
  • Live Updates: Follow SMRT or SBS Transit

🚍 Transit Tips

  • Free transfers: Switch between MRT and bus within 45 mins for same fare — no extra cost.
  • Tap before boarding: Always tap your EZ-Link or SimplyGo at the front door. Cash? Higher fare. Why pay more?
  • Real-time arrivals: Use TransitLink or MyTransport.sg to check bus arrival times at stops.
  • Express routes: Look for “Express” or “Limited Stops” buses (e.g., 36e, 51e) for faster travel during peak hours.
  • Senior/Child fares: Available for ages 65+ and 3–12 with concession card. Buy at MRT stations.

✈️ Changi Airport Shuttles & MRT Access

  • Free inter-terminal shuttles: Run 24/7 between T1–T2–T3. Pick up at Level 2 (Arrivals). No need to exit secure zone.
  • Jewel to T4: Free shuttle connects Jewel/Terminals 1–2–3 to Terminal 4 (every 10 mins, Level 1).
  • MRT Access: Terminals 2 and 3 have direct MRT stations (East-West Line). Use the free shuttle from T1 or T4.
  • Train to City: From T2/T3, take the MRT directly to City Hall (~25 mins), Orchard (~35 mins), or HarbourFront (for Sentosa).

📱 SIM + SimplyGo: Smart Combo

Use your tourist SIM with Google Maps and Grab, then pair it with SimplyGo for seamless travel.

  • Link your SIM to Apple Pay/Google Pay for contactless MRT.
  • No need to top up — just pay per ride.
  • View trip history & billing via SimplyGo Portal.
  • Cap: SGD 5.60/day (adult) on public transport.

♿ Accessibility & Family Tips

  • Wheelchair access: All MRT stations and ~95% of buses are wheelchair-friendly. Look for the wheelchair symbol for ramps, lifts, and priority boarding.
  • Tactile guidance paths: Yellow tactile tiles on floors guide visually impaired travelers from entrances to platforms. Keep them clear!
  • Hearing loops: Available at MRT station service counters and some bus interchanges for hearing aid users.
  • Priority queues: Use the dedicated lanes at station entrances — for seniors, pregnant women, and families with young kids.
  • Lost & Found: If you lose something, contact TransitLink (1800-767 8666) or ask station staff. Report lost children immediately.

💡 Pro Tips: • If traveling with a group, keep kids close — platforms are safe, but it’s easy to get separated in crowds.

🏁 You’re In! From immigration to your first MRT ride, you’ve just experienced Singapore’s legendary efficiency. Whether you're heading to Marina Bay, Choa Chu Kang, or back to Changi one day, this system will take you there — fast, clean, and without looking like a tourist.

Where to Get Good Deals

Save money while exploring Singapore! Whether you love wandering street markets or clicking through online deals, here’s how to find the best value — offline and online.

🛒 Offline Shopping

🏪 Supermarkets & Grocers

  • Cold Storage: Premium supermarket under NTUC. Great for imported goods, cheese, wine, and ready-to-eat meals. Often has promotions for cardholders.
  • Giant Hypermarket: Large format stores with wide selection. Good for weekly family shopping. Frequent discounts and bundled deals.
  • Sheng Siong: Budget-friendly chain known for fresh produce and low prices. Check for weekend flash sales.
  • Mustafa Centre: 24-hour tax-free shopping in Little India. Great for electronics, gold, spices, and souvenirs.

🥖 Bakery Closing Discounts

  • Bakery Closing Discounts: Many bakeries (e.g., BreadTalk, Food Republic, NTUC Foodfare) offer 30–70% off on bread, cakes, and buns 1–2 hours before closing (usually 7–9 PM). Ask staff or look for red discount tags.

🛍️ Street Markets & Cultural Districts

  • Explore vibrant markets like Chinatown Street Market, Bugis Street, and Geylang Serai Market for affordable fashion, souvenirs, and local snacks.
  • Visit Katong/Joo Chiat for Peranakan crafts and heritage food.

🎯 Closing-Down & Clearance Sales

  • Keep an eye out for "Closing Down Sale" signs in malls like Great World, Plaza Singapura, or Bugis+. These are often genuine and offer up to 90% off.
  • Stores relocating or shutting down (e.g., fashion boutiques, electronics shops) clear stock fast — perfect for deals on branded goods.
  • Check Hardware Singapore or Best Denki for end-of-line electronics discounts.

💵 $2 Stores & Value Shops

  • Value Dollar: Chain of $2 stores selling travel essentials, snacks, phone accessories, souvenirs, and household items. Great for last-minute buys. Locations in major malls and MRT stations.
  • Daiso: Japanese variety store (similar to Value Dollar) with slightly higher quality. Great for kitchenware, stationery, and cute gifts.
  • Miniso: Trendy, stylish items at low prices — think phone grips, organizers, and beauty tools.

🏬 Malls & Discount Stores

  • Sale Seasons:
    • Great Singapore Sale (GSS): May–Aug — huge discounts across stores.
    • Chinese New Year Sale: Jan–Feb — fashion, electronics, home goods.
  • Outlet Malls:
    • Alexandra Central (Fashion, Home)
    • Changi City Point (Electronics, Fashion)
    • Balestier Plaza (IT, Electronics)
  • Electronics: Compare prices at Sim Lim Square — negotiate hard!
💡 Pro Tip: Always carry small bills.

🌐 Online Shopping

🛒 Major Marketplaces

  • Shopee.sg: The most popular e-commerce platform in SG. Great for fashion, electronics, home goods. Frequent flash sales and free shipping.
  • Lazada.sg: Backed by Alibaba. Strong in electronics, appliances, and branded goods. Often has voucher deals.
  • Qoo10.sg: Niche deals on fashion, beauty, and gadgets. Good for unique finds.
  • Taobao (via SG proxy): For ultra-cheap goods from China. Use shipping proxies like Superbuy or BuuPass for delivery.
  • AliExpress: Global platform for budget fashion, electronics, and lifestyle products. Long delivery times but low prices.

🔄 Carousell (SG’s #1 Secondhand Marketplace)

  • Carousell.sg: Singapore’s go-to app for buying and selling secondhand goods — from phones and watches to furniture and concert tickets.
  • Negotiate prices directly with sellers. Look for “Meet-up” or “Shipping” options.
  • Search smart: Use keywords like "like new", "bundle deal", "urgent sale" for best bargains.
  • Pro Tip: Filter by location (e.g., “Near Me”) to avoid shipping fees.

👗 Fashion & Lifestyle

  • Uniqlo SG: High-quality basics, seasonal collections, and Airism wear. Frequent online exclusives and sales.
  • SHEIN SG: Trendy, ultra-affordable fashion. Fast shipping to SG. Great for casual wear and accessories.
  • Zalora: Local fashion hub with brands like Charles & Keith, Pedro, and more. Regular sales and style guides.
  • Mr. DIY Online: Now available online! Buy household essentials, tools, stationery, and festive decor at fixed low prices.

📦 General & International

  • Amazon Singapore: Reliable for books, electronics, imported goods, and Amazon Prime (fast delivery).
  • eBay: For rare or second-hand items. Check seller ratings and shipping costs.

📱 Food & Delivery Apps

  • GrabFood: Most popular. Look for "GrabPay" deals and first-order promos.
  • foodpanda: Competitive prices, frequent voucher codes.
  • Deliveroo: Best for Western food and premium restaurants.

🎯 Money-Saving Tips

  • Compare prices across Shopee, Lazada, and Qoo10 before buying.
  • Use ShopBack or GrabRewards for cashback.
  • Wait for 11.11 or 12.12 mega sales for best online deals.
  • Check for free shipping thresholds and use vouchers.
  • Sign up for newsletters (Uniqlo, Zalora, Mr. DIY) for exclusive member discounts.

💡 Bonus: Claim Your Tourist GST Refund (TRS)

As a tourist, you can claim back 8% GST on eligible purchases over $100 (including GST) at participating stores. Look for the “Tax Refund for Tourists” sign.

  • Get your TRS (Tax Refund Scheme) form at the store.
  • Purchase must be within 6 months of departure.
  • Present your passport, boarding pass, and receipts at the refund counter before check-in or at immigration.
  • Refund via credit card, cash, or PayNow.

Pro Tip: Keep all receipts and TRFs until you’ve claimed the refund.

Singapore Tips & Tricks

Maximize your Singapore experience with these insider tips:

  • Plan for Rain: Carry a small umbrella or raincoat. Afternoon thundershowers are common and can be sudden and heavy.
  • Master Public Transport: Download the TransitLink app. An EZ-Link or SimplyGo is essential for convenience and discounts.
  • Embrace Hawker Culture: Don't be intimidated! It's clean, delicious, and cheap. Look for busy stalls with high turnover.
  • Learn Basic Singlish: Knowing a few local terms can make interactions friendlier. Check our Glossary.
  • Respect Local Customs: Be aware of and respect local laws and customs (e.g., no chewing gum, no littering).
  • Stay Cool: Singapore is hot and humid. Air-conditioned malls, MRTs, and public transport are lifesavers. Drink plenty of water.
  • Carry Cash & Cards: While card payments are widespread, hawker centers and small vendors often prefer cash. ATMs are readily available.
  • Get a Local SIM Card: For data and local calls, consider buying a prepaid SIM from M1, Singtel, or StarHub at the airport or convenience stores.
  • Explore Beyond the CBD: Venture into neighborhoods like Tiong Bahru, Katong, or Yishun for unique experiences and local vibes.
  • Take Advantage of Freebies: Many attractions, parks, and galleries are free. See our Free Places section.
  • Visit During Sale Seasons: Time your shopping trip with the Great Singapore Sale (May-Aug) or Chinese New Year Sales (Jan-Feb) for the best deals.
  • Be Mindful of Peak Hours: Public transport is extremely crowded during morning (7:30-9:00 AM) and evening (5:30-7:30 PM) rush hours. Plan accordingly.
  • Use Grab: For taxis, food delivery, and even grocery shopping (GrabMart), Grab is a super convenient app.
  • Understand the Food Scene: Kopitiam (traditional coffee shop), Hawker Centre (large food court), Food Republic (aircon hawker), and Restaurants are different dining experiences.
  • Recycle: Use the blue bins for paper/cardboard and grey bins for plastic/metal/glass. Keep recyclables clean.
  • Be Punctual: Being on time is highly valued in Singaporean culture.
  • Know Emergency Numbers: Police/General Emergencies: 999, Ambulance/Fire: 995.

Relaxed & Realistic 5-Day Plan

This plan gives you a good taste of Singapore without the stress. Each day focuses on a specific area or theme, making travel easy and letting you soak in the experience.

Day Focus Area / Theme Activities
Day 1 Downtown Core & Marina Bay
(Iconic City Highlights)
  • Morning: Stroll through Gardens by the Bay (Supertree Grove/OCBC Skyway free, conservatories ticketed). Walk the Helix Bridge.
  • Lunch: Try famous satay or seafood at the historic Lau Pa Sat hawker centre.
  • Afternoon: Visit Merlion Park. Walk along the Singapore River towards Clarke Quay or Boat Quay.
  • Evening: Dinner/drinks at Clarke Quay or Boat Quay. Catch the Marina Bay Sands light show (Spectra).
Day 2 Sentosa Island
(Fun & Relaxation)
  • All Day: Explore Sentosa Island.
    • Morning: Enjoy Universal Studios Singapore (ticketed).
    • Lunch: Eat at Sentosa Food Republic or nearby options.
    • Afternoon/Evening: Visit the S.E.A. Aquarium (ticketed), relax at Siloso Beach, or explore the Sentosa Boardwalk. Stay for dinner or return to mainland/VivoCity.
Day 3 Historic Districts
(Culture & Cuisine)
  • Morning: Explore Chinatown. Visit the Buddha Tooth Relic Temple and Chinatown Complex Food Centre.
  • Lunch: Try local dishes at Chinatown Complex Food Centre.
  • Afternoon: Wander through Kampong Glam. See the Sultan Mosque and browse Haji Lane and Bussorah Street.
  • Evening: Dinner in Kampong Glam (e.g., Bussorah Street). End with a relaxed stroll.
Day 4 Orchard Road & Somerset
(Shopping & Leisure)
  • Morning: Shop along famous Orchard Road (e.g., Ion Orchard, Ngee Ann City). Relax in the Orchard Road Green Corridor.
  • Lunch: Quick meal at a mall food court or restaurant.
  • Afternoon: Visit cultural spots like the National Museum, Peranakan Museum, ArtScience Museum, or National Gallery Singapore (ticketed entries vary).
  • Evening: More dining options on Orchard Road. Optional nightlife at Haji Lane or Clarke Quay.
Day 5 Local Life & Nature Escape
(Neighborhood Vibes)
  • Morning: Explore Tiong Bahru (Art Deco, cafes, Tiong Bahru Market) or Katong/Joo Chiat (Peranakan culture, Katong Shophouses). Optional visit to Jewel Changi Airport (free entry).
  • Lunch: Eat at the neighborhood market or a local restaurant.
  • Afternoon: Continue exploring. Browse shops, relax in a park (Telok Ayer, Kallang Riverside). Optional quick visit to Singapore Zoo or a nature walk at Bukit Timah/MacRitchie.
  • Evening: Dinner at a new hawker centre or favorite spot. Wrap up your trip relaxed.

This plan spreads out the major attractions and gives you buffer time to enjoy each area without feeling rushed. Remember to check the latest access rules and opening hours for all attractions. Enjoy your trip, bro!

History & Leaders

Ever wondered how a tiny island with no natural resources became one of the world’s most dynamic cities? Buckle up — Singapore’s story is a rollercoaster of survival, vision, and sheer grit. From ancient legends to modern miracles, here’s how it all unfolded.

How It All Began: From Singapura to British Colony

  • Myth & Name Origins: Long before skyscrapers, locals spoke of Singapura — “Lion City” in Sanskrit. Legend says a Sumatran prince saw a lion here in the 13th century (though experts say lions never lived here — maybe it was a tiger?!). Still, the name stuck, and so did the symbol of strength.
  • Early Days: Originally inhabited by Malays, the island was part of various regional kingdoms. Stamford Raffles arrived in 1819 and saw its potential as a British trading post — strategically placed on major sea routes. He struck a deal with local rulers, and Singapore was open for business.
  • Colonial Boom: Under British rule, Singapore exploded into a bustling port. Its free-trade policy attracted merchants from China, India, Arabia, and beyond — laying the foundation for today’s multicultural society.
  • Japanese Occupation (1942–1945): A dark chapter. During WWII, Japan captured Singapore — a shock to the British Empire. The brutal period included the "Sook Ching" massacre, a traumatic memory etched into national consciousness.

Birth of a Nation: The Merlion, the Miracle, and the Leaders Who Made It

  • Merlion: Why a Fish-Lion? Fast forward to 1964 — Singapore needed a symbol. Enter the Merlion: half-lion (for its "Lion City" roots), half-fish (for its maritime origins). Designed by the Singapore Tourism Board, it became the ultimate mascot. Today, the iconic statue stands proudly at Merlion Park, spouting water like a boss.
  • Independence & Transformation (1965–Present): In a twist no one saw coming, Singapore was evicted from Malaysia in 1965. No room to fail. Under the People's Action Party (PAP) and the iron-willed Lee Kuan Yew (1959–1990), the nation went from swamps to skyscrapers in a generation. Focus? Education, public housing (hello, HDB flats!), anti-corruption, and attracting global investment.
  • The Legacy Continues: After LKY, Goh Chok Tong (1990–2004) softened the edges — more arts, more compassion. Then came Lee Hsien Loong (2004–2024), who steered Singapore into the digital age, pushing innovation, sustainability, and social resilience through crises like SARS and COVID-19.
  • Now & Next: In May 2024, Lawrence Wong took the helm as Prime Minister — a new chapter for a new generation. The PAP still leads, but the conversation is evolving: climate change, inequality, and identity in a globalized world.

Key Leaders Who Shaped Singapore

  • Lee Kuan Yew (1923–2015): The founding Prime Minister and architect of modern Singapore. Pragmatic, no-nonsense, and fiercely dedicated to survival. His policies — from bilingualism to strict laws — defined the nation’s DNA.
  • Goh Keng Swee (1918–2010): The mastermind behind Singapore’s economy and military. Built the foundations of our financial hub and defense force — the “quiet giant” of nation-building.
  • S. Rajaratnam (1915–2006): Co-founder of the PAP and voice of multiracialism. Wrote the National Pledge — “One united people…” — embedding unity in our identity.
  • Lee Hsien Loong: Prime Minister from 2004 to May 2024. A Cambridge-educated leader with military and civil service roots. Oversaw Singapore’s rise as a smart nation and global tech hub. Now serves as Coordinating Minister for National Security.

Who’s Leading Singapore Now?

Singapore has two key leadership roles: the President (Head of State, mostly ceremonial but with custodial powers over reserves and key appointments) and the Prime Minister (Head of Government, runs the day-to-day show).

  • Prime Minister: Lawrence Wong 🇸🇬
    Took office in May 2024, succeeding Lee Hsien Loong. A former economics professor and civil servant, he led Singapore’s pandemic recovery as Finance Minister and co-chair of the COVID-19 task force. Now steering the nation into a new era of climate resilience, innovation, and inclusive growth. You might spot him biking around the island or posting relatable TikToks!
  • President: Tharman Shanmugaratnam 🎓
    Elected in September 2023 in a landslide victory. A globally respected economist and former Deputy PM, he’s known for his warmth, wisdom, and deep commitment to social equity. While the presidency is non-partisan, his influence as a moral voice and guardian of national unity is growing.

The PAP continues to lead under Prime Minister Lawrence Wong, with a cabinet comprising experienced ministers handling Finance, Education, Home Affairs, National Development, and Trade and Industry. While opposition parties hold a minority of seats, the PAP remains dominant — but the future promises fresh debates and new directions.

National Symbols

  • Flag: Red for universal brotherhood, white for purity. The crescent represents a young nation on the rise, with five stars for democracy, peace, progress, justice, and equality.
  • Anthem: Majulah Singapura (“Onward Singapore”) — played at every school assembly, stirring pride in every heart.
  • Merlion: Still the unofficial icon — a reminder of humble maritime roots and bold imagination.

Medical Facilities & Health

Singapore has a world-class healthcare system, but it comes at a cost. Here's what you need to know:

Healthcare System

  • Quality: Excellent medical care, clean facilities, highly trained professionals.
  • Cost: Generally high. Without insurance, even minor procedures can be expensive.
  • Subsidies: Citizens and Permanent Residents receive heavy subsidies. Tourists pay full cost.

Types of Facilities

  • Public Hospitals: Large government-run hospitals (e.g., Singapore General Hospital, Tan Tock Seng Hospital, National University Hospital). High standard, lower cost than private (for locals).
  • Private Hospitals: More expensive but often offer shorter waiting times and private rooms (e.g., Gleneagles, Mount Elizabeth, Raffles Hospital).
  • Polyclinics: Government-run outpatient clinics offering subsidized care for locals. Tourists can visit but pay higher fees.
  • General Practitioners (GPs): Private clinics for minor illnesses/injuries. More expensive than polyclinics but convenient.
  • 24-Hour Clinics: Available for urgent care outside regular hours.

For Tourists & Expats

  • Travel Insurance: Essential. Ensure it covers medical expenses and evacuation. Without it, a hospital visit can be financially devastating.
  • Before You Travel: Check if you need any vaccinations. The Ministry of Health (MOH) website provides guidance.
  • Medication: Bring an adequate supply of any prescription medication, in original labeled containers. Check Singapore's regulations on importing medicines.
  • Pharmacies: Widely available (Watsons, Guardian, Unity). Can provide advice and over-the-counter medication.
  • Emergency: Dial 995 for Ambulance/Fire services.
  • Non-Emergency: For less urgent issues, visiting a GP or 24-hour clinic is usually faster and cheaper than the ER.

Useful Resources

  • Ministry of Health (MOH): Official health information.
  • HealthHub: Patient education resources, health assessments.
  • SingHealth: One of the largest healthcare groups, managing several hospitals and polyclinics.
  • National University Health System (NUHS): Another major healthcare group.
